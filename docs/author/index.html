This is a general overview of everything that led me to create this project.

OVERVIEW

I began writing this project halfway through my undergraduate degree in Physics. I was fascinated by the abstract world of mathematical physics and began imagining the possible consequences of certain speculative ideas in physics.
At first, they were just drafts, which I gradually structured until they became what we now know as the Journal of Terminal Sciences.
This website would not be possible without the help of my friend Paul, who was in charge of designing a significant part of the site's architecture. He is also the author of ideas such as The Database, inspired by one of the most famous works of the great Jorge Luis Borges.

FORMATTING INSPIRATIONS

Prior to the existence of JoTS as it is known today, I brainstormed how to write about speculative physics and fictional mathematics. My first idea was to write an anthology formatted as a scientific article, originally titled KosmicScape, though the content of that supposed article would be pure literature without formalism. As a science fiction lover, I hadn't considered the variety of formats used by Stanislaw Lem—that is, writing in the form of essays or reviews of nonexistent books. The idea of expanding this universe through collaboration, using a web format to publish these ideas, and writing in an encyclopedic style was inspired by Orion's Arm.
However, something that definitively changed the focus of this work was the Journal of Immaterial Sciences.
The idea of writing fictional scientific papers had already been considered thanks to Lem and OA, but structuring all of this as a fictional academic journal was clearly inspired by Immaterial Sciences. As you might notice, the naming of this work was heavily influenced by that parody journal.
The existence of the Chronicles is a testament to the early design of this universe, as I didn't want to waste all the writings I had already accumulated during the project's initial phases.

CONTENT INSPIRATIONS

Although my main motivation was to discuss scenarios where speculative physics takes center stage, it is evident that in building this universe, I would eventually borrow elements from other authors.
As previously mentioned, several elements were inspired by the works of Stanislaw Lem and Jorge Luis Borges. Still, most of the ideas emerged thanks to the talented writer Greg Egan. Concepts such as universes defined by mathematics, alternative physical laws, and observers emerging from abstract structures almost all stem from Egan's ideas.
The exploration into entirely imaginative domains was strongly inspired by the works of Ted Chiang. Those ways of extrapolating theories to their ultimate consequences and asking, "What would happen if an idea were literally true?" is exactly what I loved about Chiang.
Another relevant inspiration is the work of H.P. Lovecraft. I have a certain obsession with cosmic horror, and there are specific elements inspired by it—the fact that there is a massive reality that cannot be comprehended by any mind, and the addition of certain "extra-natural" elements are a footprint that Lovecraft and other horror writers left on this work.

REAL-LIFE INSPIRATIONS

As we have seen, JoTS is pure speculation; clearly, almost everything here is pure fiction with no real-world equivalent. Nevertheless, some general elements were heavily inspired by the ideas and results of philosophers, scientists, and mathematicians.
Most importantly, the Theoretical Space—the canvas where all possibilities manifest—originates from two concepts in mathematical logic and physics.

The primary catalyst for structuring the multiverse was an article by Michael Heller titled "Multiverse—Too Much of Not Enough?", which proposes an "esoteric" version of the multiverse based on category theory. In this model, every possible consequence of category theory exists in some universe. That is, a landscape where not only all possible physics and mathematics are realized, but all forms of logic. This gives us enormous freedom to write about basically any scientific or philosophical idea we might have.
The fact that there are mutually exclusive, completely separate, and irreducible universes comes as a consequence of Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems. In fact, the existence of certain brute facts and the impossibility of unified frameworks is a very high-level extrapolation of the incompleteness theorems.

Now, the Proofscape is conceived solely to justify the leap between universes. It arises from an extrapolation of Tarski's undefinability theorem, in the sense that there should be a higher theory that defines the truth (or rather, the truths) of the entire Theoretical Space. It also justifies the fact that one can travel through these universes via an "indirect" connection thanks to the First Theory; the rest of the Proofscape is merely a consequence of the theorem. All things are realized in the Theoretical Space, meaning nothing interesting can occur within the Proofscape itself. The status of Zoi zoi (an idea of Paul's, though I liked it), and other similar beings like Laplace's Demon, have specific rationales to justify them.

The idea of universes where copies of the Theoretical Space (or even the Proofscape) are "realized" is both an extrapolation of the Reflection Principle and an inspiration from Borges.
And I can attest that other interesting ideas emerged primarily from scientific and logical-mathematical results.

Heller, M. (2019). Multiverse—Too Much or Not Enough? Universe, 5(5), 113. https://doi.org/10.3390/universe5050113

OTHER INSPIRATIONS

I wouldn't want to leave out other inspirations that, in one way or another, contributed their grain of sand. Among these are Douglas Adams, Arthur C. Clarke, Liu Cixin, Isaac Asimov, John W. Campbell, and various speculative evolution projects.
